Section 1

Comparing Adjectives

Forming comparative and superlative adjectives, context rules, and modifier variations.

Comparing Adjectives Form Rules Table

Adjective Type Base Example Comparative Pattern Superlative Pattern
One syllable hard adjective + -er → harder the + adjective + -est → the hardest
One syllable ending in -e nice adjective + -r → nicer the + adjective + -st → the nicest
One syllable ending in vowel + consonant fat double last consonant + -er → fatter the + double last consonant + -est → the fattest
Two syllables ending in -y happy adjective -y + -ier → happier the + adjective -y + -iest → the happiest
Two or more syllables enjoyable more + adjective → more enjoyable the most + adjective → the most enjoyable
Irregular Forms good, bad, far better, worse, further/farther the best, the worst, the furthest/farthest

Comparative Adjectives Usage

Usage 1: Comparing Two or More Items / People / Places

"Younger runners will always be faster than older runners."

Usage 2: Same Entity at Two Different Times

"I'm much fitter than I was last year."

The "Than" Clause Context Rule

We use than after comparatives. However, we can leave out the than-clause if clear from context!

"Older athletes are getting faster and fitter." (understood: than in the past)

Superlative Adjectives & Modifiers

Usage: One item compared to ALL others in a group

"The Olympics is probably the most exciting sports event in the sports calendar."

Modifying Superlatives:

• one of the / some of the + superlative + plural noun

"It's one of the few chances we get to see some of the best athletes in the world..."

"Tamsin is one of the most generous people I know."

• ordinal numbers

"Our team was the third best in the competition."

Replacing "the" with possessive

the best friend → my best friend

the greatest achievement → his greatest achievement

Section 2

Comparing Adverbs

Comparing how actions/events are performed using regular adverbs, flat adverbs, and irregulars.

Standard Adverbs

Use more / most + adverb

"Runners aged 50 and over are speeding up more rapidly than younger people."

"Women aged 60 to 68 improved the most markedly."

Same Form as Adjectives

Adverbs like hard, fast, straight, late, early, quick add -er / -est.

"Women aged 60 to 68 run on average four minutes faster each year."

Irregular Adverbs

Key list: well → better → best, badly → worse → worst, far → further → furthest, little → less → least.

"I did worse than I had expected in the exam, so I was disappointed."

Section 3

Other Ways of Comparing

Opposites, emphasis modifiers, similarity, repeating comparatives, and parallel variation.

Opposites: Less & The Least

We use less / the least to mean the opposite of more / the most.

"You might imagine that the Masters Games would be less exciting to watch."

"That was probably the least enjoyable meal I've ever had!"

Adding Emphasis to Comparatives

Informal / General:

even, far, a great deal, a little, a lot, much + comparative

"Older women showed much greater increases..."

Formal English:

slightly, considerably, significantly + comparative

"Figures for 2003 were significantly higher than 2000."

"...was only slightly lower than men."

CRITICAL WARNING: No "VERY"

We CANNOT use very with comparative adjectives or adverbs!

❌ NOT: the number of women was very lower

✔ CORRECT: ...was much lower / significantly lower

Similarity: As ... As & Multipliers

• Same/Similar: as + adj/adv + as

"My car is as old as yours." (= same age)

"Older athletes are as likely to achieve..."

• Adding modifiers / multipliers: just, almost, nearly, half, twice, three times + as ... as

"...team was almost as successful as in 2003."

"He can run twice as fast as the others..."

• Different: not as + adj/adv + as

"While they may not be as fast as their younger counterparts..."

Change Over Time (Repeating)

Show continuous change by repeating the comparative structure.

"Each year athletes seem to be getting better and better."

"Our atmosphere is gradually becoming more and more polluted."

"It seems less and less likely that there will be an election..."

Parallel Variation: The ... The ...

We use the + comparative ..., the + comparative ... to show two things vary/change at the same time.

"It would seem that the longer athletes keep competing the greater their chances of setting new records are."

"The sooner the better."

Section 4

Comparing Quantities

Quantifiers for countable and uncountable nouns, emphasis, parity, and quantity multipliers.

Comparing Quantities Table

Base Quantifier Comparative Superlative
a lot / much / many more the most
a few (Countable) fewer (+ plural countable noun) the fewest (+ plural countable noun)
a little (Uncountable) less (+ uncountable noun) the least (+ uncountable noun)

Countable vs Uncountable Quantity Rules

• More / Most (Plural or Uncountable):

"Today's top sportspeople receive a lot more money than in the past."

• Fewer / Fewest (Plural Countable Nouns):

"25 years ago few 60-year-old men and even fewer women would have considered running a marathon."

• Less / Least (Uncountable Nouns):

"There used to be less information available about fitness."

Quantity Emphasis, Multipliers & Parity

• Emphasis with Countable Nouns:

a lot / many + more / fewer + plural noun

"...given today's athletes many more opportunities to succeed."

• Emphasis with Uncountable Nouns:

a lot / much + more / less + uncountable noun

"Athletes need to do much more training than in the past."

• Repeating Quantity Comparatives:

"So much in our society is about making more and more money."

• Parity & Specific Multipliers:

"There aren't as many people doing sports..."

"China won nearly twice as many silver medals as the US."

"The US won more than three times as many medals as Great Britain."

Grammar Extra

Comparing Nouns

Using "like", "the same (as)", and "similar to" to compare how similar things are.

The Same (As)

To express identical degree, status, or identity.

"Older athletes can achieve the same degree of physical improvement as those in their twenties and thirties."

Like + Noun

To compare character, similarity of action or manner.

"He swims like a fish."

Similar To

To show key resemblance between items.

"This film is similar to this director's last one."

Native Speaker Intuition & Mental Models

Why does English structure comparisons this way? Visual explanations of native speaker logic.

Logical Rule 1

Why can't native speakers say "very lower"?

🧠 Native Speaker Mental Model:

1. "Very" is a static point-pinning word. It attaches to absolute states (e.g. "very cold", "very fast").

2. A Comparative (-er / more) is NOT a static point—it is a vector/movement along a directional scale.

3. Therefore, to modify a movement vector, native speakers need a distance multiplier (e.g. much lower, far faster, slightly lower, significantly higher) instead of a static point pin like "very".

❌ Static "Very" + Vector "Lower"
✔ Distance Multiplier "Much" + Vector "Lower"
Logical Rule 2

The "Linked Sliders" (The... the...)

🧠 Native Speaker Mental Model:

When native speakers say "The longer athletes compete, the greater their chances...", they visualize two synchronized sound-board audio sliders.

• As Slider A ("the longer") moves UP, Slider B ("the greater") automatically rises in direct correlation.

Slider A: Length of Competing The longer...
Slider B: Chance of New Records ...the greater!
Logical Rule 3

Discrete Digits vs Continuous Fluid (Fewer vs Less)

🧠 Native Speaker Mental Model:

• Countable (Fewer) = Visualized as distinct individual blocks or marbles (fewer women, fewer medals).

• Uncountable (Less) = Visualized as an indivisible continuous mass or volume of fluid (less information, less training, less money).

Distinct Marbles
Fewer Women
Continuous Fluid
Less Information
